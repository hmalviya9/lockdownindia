# India is not going to lock down next month.
# It's going to do something more interesting — and most analysts will miss it.

*File № 003 · Captured 2026-04-23*

---

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched an air war on Iran and assassinated its supreme leader. Iran's reply was the Strait of Hormuz — twenty-one ship attacks, sea mines, two vessels seized as recently as Wednesday — and roughly twenty per cent of the world's seaborne oil stopped moving. The IEA called the resulting shortfall the largest supply disruption in history.

Naturally, the lockdown rumour started.

Bangladesh shut its universities, brought Eid holidays forward, set up a fuel-card system, and posted security forces at three thousand petrol stations. Pakistan moved its government to a four-day week. Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Bhutan, Slovakia, Slovenia, South Africa and Kenya are rationing. The pattern is so loud that *#LockdownInIndia* has been trending for weeks.

Here is the thing the trend doesn't know.

**India spent the last sixty days getting ready.**

Not in a press conference. In the import data. By March, seventy per cent of India's crude was no longer transiting Hormuz — Russia's share alone surged ninety-four per cent month-on-month to 2.06 million barrels a day, after Washington quietly granted a waiver. India's strategic reserves now buy seventy-four days of cover. Bangladesh has roughly zero. The Modi government cut petrol excise from thirteen rupees to three, and diesel from ten to nothing — fiscal absorption instead of demand suppression. Petroleum Minister Hardeep Puri has called the lockdown rumour "completely false" so many times the denials are themselves a data point.

The futures curve agrees with him. EIA expects Brent to peak in the second quarter near a hundred and fifteen dollars and then ease. CFTC's managed-money positioning is two-to-one long. The market is pricing recovery, not collapse. The people who would actually lose money on a wrong call are not betting on a crash.

So no. No lockdown.

But.

India's LPG strategic storage is *five days*. Not sixty. Five. The country is the world's second-largest LPG importer, household kitchens hang on imports, and the Hormuz LPG corridor is the same corridor the missiles fly over. That is the soft spot, and Delhi knows it.

What's coming, in the next thirty days, is not a lockdown. It's the precision version of one — the kind that doesn't make a headline until the third week.

Watch for: an LPG cylinder quota dressed up as a "fair distribution scheme." A "work from home advisory" for non-essential staff in Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore — Pakistan-style, not Wuhan-style. One or two states quietly declaring an energy emergency before the Centre does. Aviation fuel curbs that thin the domestic flight schedule. Possibly a weekend movement curb in a single metro if Hormuz fully closes a third time.

Six triggers tell you when it tips harder. Hormuz shut for thirty continuous days. Iran strikes Saudi or UAE infrastructure. Brent above one-thirty for two weeks. US troops on Iranian soil. India's strategic reserve drops below thirty days. An attack on an Indian refinery — Reliance Jamnagar would buy you the lockdown in seventy-two hours.

None of those have fired yet. Watch them like instruments on a dashboard, not narrators in a panic.

The lockdown story is the one everyone is rushing to tell. It will be wrong, and the people who tell it will lose the credibility they spent the last six years building.

The right story is the one nobody is telling. **India is rationing the next thirty days from above, not below — through cabinet decisions, not chowkidars at your gate.** The country isn't going dark. It's going careful. And the people who see it coming will be the ones reading the import manifests, not the press releases.

That's the dossier we're building. Every number, sourced. No vibes.
